Life history and reproduction of Schmitzia hiscockiana sp. nov.
(Rhodophyta, Gigartinales) from the British Isles
CHRISTINE A. MAGGS' AND MICHAEL D. GUIRY
Department ofBotany, National University of Ireland,
University College, Galway, Ireland
c.A. MAGGS AND M.D. GUIRY. 1985. Life history and reproduction of Schmitzia hiscockiana sp.
nov. (Rhodophyta, Gigartinales) from the British Isles.
24: 297-310.
A complanate Schmitzia species (Calosiphoniaceae) has been found growing on subtidal cobbles in
the British Isles. The ephemeral erect plants are uniaxial, filamentous and gelatinous and bear
spermatangia and three-celled carpogonial branches in June/July and carposporophytes from late
July to September. Carpospores formed sterile crustose plants and loosely-organized filaments bearing
tetrasporangia in culture at 15°C, 16 : 8 h (long day, LD). Obliquely cruciate to irregularly zonate
tetrasporangia were formed on the crustose plants after transfer to 10 or 15°C, 8: 1 h (short day,
SD). At 15°C LD some crusts, which may have been the result of inoculation of filaments from the
parent gametophytes rather than germinated carpospores, formed erect filaments which grew poorly.
On transfer to SD, however, normal erect axes were formed and these have not reproduced. Tet­
raspores released in culture formed discs which gave rise to erect axes at 10 or 15°C, SD, but not at
LD. Erect axes were similar in construction to field gametophytes, but no reproduction was observed.
Tetraspores from field-collected crusts grew to form discs bearing erect axes indistinguishable from
those obtained from cultured tetrasporophytes. Schmitzia hiscockiana is superficially most similar
to Schmitzia evanescens Hawkes (New Zealand) and S japonica Okamura (Japan), but differs from
these species in features such as branching patter, number of whorl-branchlets and number of cells
in the carpogonial branch. The carpogonial branch resembles that ofSchmitzia neapo/itana (Berthold)
P. Silva (North Atlantic, Mediterranean) and it is suggested that S. hiscockiana and S. neapolitana
are closely related despite their difference in habit.
